Why Your Hiring Data Belongs in a Spreadsheet

A folder of PDF CVs is an archive. You can open files, read them, and close them — but you can't sort by experience level, filter by skill, or rank by relevance. You can't share a "candidate database" without emailing thirty individual attachments. You can't compare two candidates side by side without toggling between windows.

A CSV transforms that same information into a database you can actually work with. Every candidate is a row. Every piece of information is a column. One file, shareable instantly, filterable in seconds, importable into any tool you use.

3 Ways Small Businesses Use the CSV Export

Collaborative Shortlisting

Import the CSV into a shared Google Sheet. Add columns for "Reviewer 1 Score," "Reviewer 2 Score," and "Notes." Share the sheet with everyone involved in the decision. Each person reviews the top-ranked candidates and adds their scores. Average the columns. Your shortlist is a formula away. No meetings required. No emailing PDFs back and forth.

Building a Talent Pool

Not every strong candidate gets the role. Keep a folder of CSV exports from past hiring rounds — one file per role. When a similar position opens in six months, search your stored candidate data before posting a new job. Your next best hire might already be in your talent pool, having applied before. Re-engage before spending anything on new sourcing.

Tracking Your Hiring Pipeline

Add a "Status" column to your spreadsheet with values like Applied, Screening, Phone Screen, Interview, Offer, and Hired. Update it as candidates progress. You've built a basic ATS in a spreadsheet — free, portable, and exactly as complex as you need it to be.
